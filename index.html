SECONROB: Self-Controlled Robotic Inspection
Using Acoustic Emission Sensing

Author One1, Author Two1, Author Three2

1Acoustic Control Systems  |  2Partner Institution

    Abstract

    This page presents the key results of the SECONROB project demonstrated at the DGZfP 2026 annual conference. The project investigates autonomous robotic inspection strategies that combine acoustic emission sensing with self-learning control algorithms to detect structural defects in safety-critical components.

    The proposed system achieves reliable defect localisation under realistic field conditions without manual intervention, reducing inspection cycle times by up to 40 % compared to conventional approaches.

    Motivation & Background

    Non-destructive testing (NDT) of large structures such as pressure vessels, pipelines and wind-turbine blades typically requires skilled personnel and results in long downtime periods. Autonomous robotic inspection offers a path to continuous, low-cost monitoring.

    Acoustic emission (AE) sensing is particularly attractive because it is passive, broadband, and sensitive to crack growth and impact events. Coupling AE with a mobile robot creates a system that can cover large surface areas without contact limitations.

    Methods

    The inspection robot carries an array of piezoelectric AE sensors mounted on a compliant coupling frame. Signals are digitised at 2 MS/s and fed in real time to an on-board FPGA that performs hit detection, feature extraction (rise time, peak amplitude, energy) and source localisation via time-difference-of-arrival (TDOA) triangulation.

    A reinforcement-learning path planner continuously updates the robot trajectory to minimise residual source-localisation uncertainty, guiding the sensor array towards regions of highest acoustic activity.

    Results

    Experiments were conducted on a 2 m × 1 m steel plate with artificially introduced notch defects. The system was benchmarked against a stationary eight-sensor array of equal aperture.

    ± 8 mm Localisation accuracy (median)
    40 % Reduction in inspection time
    97.3 % Hit detection rate

    Conclusion

    The SECONROB system demonstrates that adaptive robotic path planning combined with real-time AE processing significantly improves localisation accuracy and throughput compared to fixed sensor networks. The approach scales to larger structures and can be extended to multi-robot deployments.

    Future work will focus on transfer to curved surfaces (pipes and vessels), integration of guided-wave excitation, and in-situ validation on operational plant equipment.
